Arduino System for monitoring voltage and water quality of a Dual-Chambered Microbial Fuel Cell

Project: ACSEF 2020 Fair Submission
Awards: Project Planet Special Award, 1st Place ACSEF, Honorary Mention CSEF
Duration: Approximately 5 months (Mid September 2020 - Early February 2020)
Objective: To measure minute amounts of voltage generated by the best form of a microbial fuel cell while determining water quality with pH and turbidity sensors using Arduino.
Type of Project: Solo Project without Mentorship

-

This project measures minute amounts of voltage while determining water quality with pH and turbidity sensors. The data was collected through the Arduino-microcontroller, as the MFC generated electricity by respiration with ROETECH-106PS, a concentrated mixture of spore-forming and vegetative bacteria, where Bacillus and Pseudomonas species provide a multi enzyme producing culture. A dual chamber design was implemented to collect sensor data. After two models of an Arduino-monitored MFC where the circuit could not measure voltage due to low bit resolution, a divider circuit with external resistance of 32k and 1.5V DC voltage was added to increase resolution from 4.88mV/bit to 0.75mV/bit, allowing Arduino to read mV under 1.5V. After testing acetic acids within the cathode and different concentrations of salt within the bridge, the most improved outcome functioned with 400g of salt/1000mL distilled water for cathode; 900mL ROETECH-106PS, 100 mL distilled water, 25g of sugar for anode; 2.5g agar and 10g salt in 25mL distilled water for the bridge. Arduino’s data showed a peak at 260mV 10 hours in, which the multimeter read as 272mV, a starting pH of 5.45 and ending at 6.31, as turbidity stayed relatively constant at 3.16NTU. The anode’s wastewater has accumulated on the top layer, the clean water but with debris on the bottom. By testing accuracy through a multimeter and comparing readings to Arduino’s data, there was an average error ranging from 10mV-20mV, an insignificant difference. Arduino can monitor 5 cells at once and costs less than 1% of the price of a commercial multimeter, making this cost effective and environmentally efficient.
